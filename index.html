The AI Usage Hierarchy

Ten levels of depth — from simple lookup to ontological shift — each unlocking a fundamentally different relationship with thinking itself.

↓ deeper leverage    surface use ↑
01
Retrieval
Factual Lookup
Using AI as a faster search engine — "What is X?" / "How do I Y?"
Example Prompts
"What is the capital of Bhutan?"
"How do I remove a background in Photoshop?"
"What's the difference between LLC and S-Corp?"
What's Happening
You already know the question. You just need the answer delivered. AI is a reference desk — faster than Google, more conversational than a wiki. The question is fully formed before you arrive.
02
Procedure
Step-by-Step Guidance
Not just "what" but "walk me through it" — sequenced instruction.
Example Prompts
"Walk me through setting up a Next.js project from scratch."
"Give me a step-by-step plan for preparing for a marathon in 16 weeks."
"How do I negotiate a raise — what do I say and in what order?"
What's Happening
You know the destination but not the path. You're asking AI to decompose a goal into a sequence. The shift from Level 1: you're trusting AI with the ordering and chunking of knowledge, not just the knowledge itself.
03
Application
Situational Advice
"Given my situation, what should I do?" — contextual judgment.
Example Prompts
"I have $50K saved, I'm 34, no debt. Should I invest in index funds or pay down my mortgage faster?"
"My cofounder and I disagree on pricing. Here's the context… What would you recommend?"
"I got two job offers. Here are the details. What should I weigh?"
The Shift
You're no longer asking for generic knowledge — you're feeding in your specific situation and asking AI to act as an advisor. This requires trust in AI's judgment, not just its memory.
— from answers to thinking —
04
Clarification
Problem Articulation
"Help me figure out what I'm actually trying to solve."
Example Prompts
"Something feels off about our product-market fit but I can't articulate what. Can you ask me questions to help me get clarity?"
"I keep procrastinating on this project and I don't know why. Help me figure out what's really going on."
"I have a vague sense our team structure is wrong. Help me name the actual problem."
The Shift
You arrive without a clear question. The value of AI here is that it helps you formulate the question itself. This is a qualitative leap — you're using AI not because you lack answers, but because you lack clarity on what to even ask.
05
Strategy
Framework & Mental Model Selection
"How should I even think about this?" — choosing the lens.
Example Prompts
"What frameworks exist for thinking about whether to build vs. buy?"
"I need to make a decision about my career. What are the different ways someone could think about a decision like this — not the answer, but the approach?"
"Is this a prioritization problem, a resource-allocation problem, or a strategy problem? Help me figure out what kind of problem it is."
The Shift
You're not asking for the answer or even the question — you're asking for the thinking tool. Which model, framework, or discipline should you use to approach the problem? AI becomes a navigator across fields of thought.
06
Reasoning
Thinking Partnership
"Think with me" — real-time collaborative reasoning.
Example Prompts
"Let me talk through my reasoning on this. Push back where my logic is weak and help me strengthen the argument."
"I'm going to share my draft strategy. Steelman it, then try to break it."
"I think the root cause is X. Play devil's advocate — what am I not seeing?"
The Shift
AI is no longer a vendor of answers. It's a sparring partner. You're generating ideas and using AI as a stress-test, a mirror, a second mind. The value is in the dynamic — the back-and-forth — not any single output.
— from thinking to seeing —
07
Metacognition
Examining Your Own Thinking
"What patterns, biases, or blind spots are showing up in how I think?"
Example Prompts
"Look at the last five decisions I've described to you. What patterns do you notice in how I make decisions?"
"I tend to frame everything as an optimization problem. What might I be missing by defaulting to that lens?"
"What cognitive biases might be at play in the way I just described my situation?"
The Shift
You're turning the lens on yourself. Not "help me think about the problem" but "help me see how I think." AI becomes a mirror for your cognition — revealing defaults, patterns, and biases you can't see from inside them.
08
Assumptions
Surfacing Hidden Beliefs
"What am I assuming that I don't even know I'm assuming?"
Example Prompts
"Based on everything I've told you, what unstated assumptions am I operating from?"
"I keep coming back to the same conclusion. What beliefs would have to be true for that conclusion to hold? Are any of them shaky?"
"What's the thing I seem to be taking as a given that might not actually be true?"
The Shift
This goes beneath conscious thought patterns into the substrate of belief. You're asking AI to excavate premises you didn't know you held — the invisible load-bearing walls of your reasoning. Changing one of these can restructure everything above it.
09
Identity
Examining "Who I'm Being"
"What identity or role am I operating from — and is it serving me?"
Example Prompts
"When I describe this conflict with my team, who am I being? A victim? A hero? A fixer? Help me see the role I've cast myself in."
"I notice I always position myself as the underdog in my business. What would change if I stopped identifying that way?"
"What kind of leader do my decisions reveal me to be — not the kind I say I want to be, but the kind I'm actually being?"
The Shift
Below assumptions lie identities — the self-concept from which all assumptions, frameworks, and questions naturally arise. "I'm a scrappy underdog" produces entirely different questions than "I'm a market leader." AI helps you see the character you're unconsciously playing.
10
Ontological
Seeing How the World Occurs to You
"Help me see the water I'm swimming in — the reality I've constructed that I mistake for reality itself."
Example Prompts
"I realize every question I've asked you today presupposes that my business is a problem to be solved. What if it's not? What would my questions even be if I saw it differently?"
"I experience my life as a series of obligations. Help me see what worldview makes that feel true — and what an alternative world might look like."
"I'm not asking you to solve anything. I'm asking you to help me see the entire frame I've been operating inside, so I can step outside of it."
The Shift
This is the deepest level. You're not working on a problem — you're examining the ontological context from which all problems, identities, assumptions, and thinking arise. The question isn't "what should I do?" or even "who am I being?" — it's "what is the nature of the world as it occurs to me, and how does that occurrence generate everything downstream?" A shift here doesn't solve problems. It dissolves them.
Each level contains every level below it. You can enter at any point — but the deeper you go, the more everything above transforms.
